## The concept

Amazon's product development process starts with a press release. Before any code is written or design is made, the team writes a mock press release announcing the finished product — as if it's launch day and the product is perfect.

This forces clarity on three questions:
1. Who is this for?
2. What does it do for them that they couldn't do before?
3. Why will they care?

If you can't write the press release, you don't understand the product well enough to build it.

## Why it works

Most product teams start from the inside out: we have this capability, what can we build with it? Amazon inverts this. They start with the customer experience and work backwards to the capability.

The press release is a forcing function. It makes vague ideas concrete. It surfaces disagreements early — before they're expensive.

## My adaptation

I use a simplified version when evaluating whether a credit feature is worth building:

> *"Today, [customer] can finally [do X] without [Y friction]. For the first time, [outcome they care about]."*

If I can't complete that sentence in a way that's compelling, the feature probably isn't ready to be built.

## Limitation

Working Backwards can be gamed. Teams learn to write good press releases for bad ideas. The quality of the method depends entirely on the quality of the customer insight behind it.

It's a tool for clarity, not a substitute for deep understanding of the user.
