## The concept

A second-order effect is what happens as a consequence of the first-order change. Most policy decisions are made based on first-order thinking. The second-order effects are where things get interesting — and where most policies fail.

## Applied to credit

**First-order:** Tighten the credit scoring cutoff → fewer risky borrowers get credit.

**Second-order:** The marginal borrowers you rejected go to informal lenders at 36% interest. Their financial distress increases. They stop paying other EMIs. NPAs rise anyway, just not on your book.

**Another example:**

**First-order:** Offer a loan restructuring option → reduce immediate defaults.

**Second-order:** Borrowers who could repay begin to strategically restructure. You've created a moral hazard. The population that uses the option shifts toward willful defaulters.

## How I use this

Before finalizing any credit policy change, I now force myself to ask: *"What will the borrower do next?"* Not what we want them to do — what they'll actually do given the incentives we're creating.

The borrower is always optimizing for their situation. The best lending policies account for that.

## Use this when

Evaluating any change to approval rates, pricing, collection strategy, or product design in a financial product. The first-order effect is almost always obvious. The second-order effects are where the real risk lives.
