## The problem they were solving

Buy Now Pay Later promised to replace the credit card for the next generation of borrowers — younger, credit-thin, suspicious of revolving debt. The pitch was compelling: zero-interest, transparent installments, no hidden fees.

Between 2019 and 2022, BNPL grew explosively. Klarna, Affirm, Afterpay, and a dozen Indian clones raised billions. Then, in 2022–23, the defaults came.

## What went wrong

**The underwriting was thin.** BNPL lenders underwrite at the point of purchase — a fundamentally different context than traditional credit. The borrower who buys a ₹8,000 phone in an enthusiastic moment is not the same person who will have ₹8,000 available in 30 days.

Traditional credit risk models were built on bureau data, income verification, and time. BNPL lenders didn't have most of that. They substituted it with behavioral signals (device type, browsing history, cart value) that turned out to be weak predictors when economic conditions shifted.

**Stacking was invisible.** Because BNPL loans often don't appear on credit bureaus, a borrower could take 4–5 simultaneous BNPL lines across different providers, none of which knew about the others. The aggregate exposure was invisible to any single lender.

**The customer segment was fragile.** BNPL disproportionately served the thin-file, entry-level borrower. When inflation rose and disposable incomes tightened in 2022, this segment was the first to break.

## What the models missed

1. **Stress testing against macro shocks.** Models were trained on a low-rate, low-inflation environment. They had never seen rising rates and job insecurity simultaneously.

2. **Behavioral drift at scale.** Early adopters of BNPL were self-selected responsible borrowers who understood the product. As BNPL went mainstream, the population shifted — and the model didn't know it.

3. **Emotional spending is high-risk spending.** Underwriting a purchase made at the checkout screen, in an excited moment, is fundamentally different from underwriting a planned credit application.

## The lesson for Indian fintech

India's BNPL players largely avoided the worst of this because RBI tightened the regulatory screws early (the 2022 BNPL PPI circular). But the underlying risk logic hasn't changed.

Any credit product targeting thin-file borrowers, underwriting at the moment of purchase, and offering sub-30-day tenors is carrying risks that won't show up in the model until it's too late.

The question isn't whether those borrowers should get credit. They should. The question is whether the underwriting and pricing models are honest about the risk they're taking on.
